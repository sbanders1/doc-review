Expert Report of Margaret H. Chen, PhD
Prepared in the matter of Robertson v. Halloway

I have been retained by counsel for the plaintiff, Eleanor Robertson, to provide expert opinion regarding the condition of the premises at 412 Linden Street on the date of the incident, June 14, 2024.

I hold a Ph.D. in horticultural science from the University of California, Davis.
I have served as a consulting and testifying expert in matters involving residential turf maintenance and premises safety since 2009.
I have testified in fourteen prior matters in state and federal courts.
A copy of my curriculum vitae is attached as Exhibit A.

In forming my opinions I reviewed the following materials.
The Riverdale Police Department incident report dated June 14, 2024.
The plaintiff's medical records from St. Mary's Urgent Care Clinic.
Photographs of the premises taken by the plaintiff on the day of the incident.
Photographs taken during my own site inspection on August 3, 2024.
The deposition transcripts of the plaintiff and the defendant, Thomas Halloway.
The municipal code of the City of Riverdale, Section 8-114, governing residential property maintenance.
A weather report for Riverdale covering May and June of 2024.

I conducted an in-person inspection of the premises at 412 Linden Street on August 3, 2024.
At the time of my inspection, the lawn had recently been cut.
However, photographic evidence preserves the condition of the lawn on the date of the incident.

Based on my review and inspection, I have determined the following.
The lawn at 412 Linden Street had not been mowed for at least nine consecutive weeks prior to the incident.
The grass on the date of the incident was approximately fourteen inches in height.
The expected height of a residential lawn under normal maintenance is between two and three inches.
The grass species present at the property is tall fescue, which during the relevant period had a typical growth rate of approximately one inch per week given local rainfall.
The plaintiff, a forty-eight year old letter carrier for the United States Postal Service, was delivering mail to the residence on June 14, 2024.
The walkway from the public sidewalk to the front porch of the residence was overgrown such that the concrete path was substantially obscured.
A protruding root from a maple tree adjacent to the walkway, ordinarily visible to a pedestrian, was concealed by the unmown grass.
The plaintiff's right foot caught on this concealed root, causing her to fall forward.
The plaintiff sustained a Grade II ankle sprain and lateral contusions to her right knee, requiring medical care and resulting in eight weeks of restricted duty.

The defendant Thomas Halloway has owned and resided at 412 Linden Street since 2017.
In his deposition the defendant testified that he had been on an extended trip out of state from late March through mid-June of 2024.
The defendant further testified that he had not arranged for any lawn care during his absence.
The defendant did not engage a property manager or service to maintain the premises during this period.
The defendant returned home on June 16, 2024, two days after the incident.

Section 8-114 of the Riverdale Municipal Code prohibits residential grass from exceeding eight inches in height.
The City of Riverdale issued no fewer than three notices of violation to the defendant during the period in question.
These notices were addressed to the property and went unanswered.

It is my opinion, held to a reasonable degree of professional certainty, that the condition of the lawn at 412 Linden Street on June 14, 2024 represented a foreseeable hazard to any pedestrian lawfully traversing the walkway.
A landowner exercising reasonable care over residential premises in an urban neighborhood would be expected to maintain the grass at a height that does not conceal fixed objects on or near pedestrian pathways.
The defendant's failure to arrange for any maintenance of the premises over a nine week period constitutes a substantial departure from this standard.
Had the lawn been maintained at a reasonable height, the protruding tree root would have been visible to the plaintiff.
The plaintiff would in that case have been able to step over or around the root in the ordinary course of her duties.
The plaintiff's fall and resulting injuries are therefore a direct and foreseeable consequence of the defendant's failure to maintain the premises.

I reserve the right to supplement this report should additional materials become available.
